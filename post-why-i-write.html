Why I Started Writing: On Building a Personal Brand as a Student

📅 April 28, 2025 ✍️ Muhammad Syamil ☕ 5 min read

For a long time, I convinced myself I had nothing worth saying. I was a student, not an expert. Who was I to write about technology, design, or software development? Surely readers would prefer content from people who actually know things.

Then a professor said something that changed my perspective: "If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough." He wasn't talking about writing. He was talking about code reviews. But I applied it to writing anyway.

"The expert is the person who has made all the mistakes possible in a very narrow field." — Niels Bohr

What Writing Actually Does

Writing forces clarity. When I understand something in my head, it feels complete. When I try to explain it in writing, the gaps appear immediately. Where does my understanding actually stop? What am I assuming? What would a reader need to know that I'm skipping?

This is the real value of a learning blog — not the audience (which may be tiny), but the act of writing itself. You are your most important reader.

The Personal Brand Myth

"Personal brand" sounds exhausting and performative. It conjures images of LinkedIn posts that begin with "Hot take:" and end with "Thoughts?". That's not what I mean.

What I mean is simpler: a consistent, findable record of who you are and what you think. When someone Googles your name before a job interview, a well-maintained blog tells a better story than a sparse LinkedIn profile.

It signals initiative. It signals that you can communicate. It gives a hiring manager something to ask you about beyond your grades.

The Vulnerability Problem

The biggest obstacle isn't technical. It's the fear that someone will read your writing and conclude that you're not as smart as you're pretending to be.

Here's the thing: most readers are kind. Most people who find a student's learning blog are rooting for the writer. The imposter syndrome that tells you "you're not qualified" is lying.

The correct frame is: you're not writing as an authority, you're writing as a learner. Those are different voices, and both are valuable.

What I Wish I'd Known Earlier

Start before you're ready. I could have begun this blog two years ago. Everything I would have written then would have been less polished than now — and that's fine. The posts from two years ago would have served readers two years behind me.

Publish regularly, even if imperfectly. Consistency matters more than quality in the early days. You can't edit a blank page.

Write for your past self. The most useful blog posts are answers to questions you had six months ago. If you were confused about something, someone else is confused about it right now.

Starting Is the Hard Part

So if you've been thinking about starting a blog, a GitHub portfolio, a newsletter, or even a series of LinkedIn posts — the moment you're waiting for isn't coming. The moment is now. You already know enough to help someone.

🖊️ Practical tip: Start with a "Today I Learned" post. Write one thing you learned this week. Keep it under 300 words. Publish it. That's it. That's the start.
